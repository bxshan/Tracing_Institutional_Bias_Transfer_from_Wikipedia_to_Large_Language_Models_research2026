Hopkins School ORG is a private, college-preparatory, coeducational, day school for grades 7–12 located in New Haven GPE , Connecticut GPE .
In 1660, Edward Hopkins PERSON , seven-time governor of the Connecticut Colony ORG , bequeathed a portion of his estate to found schools dedicated to "the breeding up of hopeful youths." == History ==


=== Founding ===
John Davenport PERSON , a founder of the New Haven Colony ORG , was an early proponent of education in the colony. Grammar schools of the time generally prepared young men for college, but the Puritan GPE colony was too far from England GPE for its citizens to attend the existing English schools. Parents of the time were generally more concerned with spending their money on essentials such as food, and viewed formal education as an extravagance most could not afford. Davenport GPE enlisted the help of a friend, Edward Hopkins PERSON , governor of the Connecticut Colony ORG , to found a traditional grammar school that would teach Latin, Greek NORP , and Hebrew grammar. The school's first home was a building on the New Haven Green ORG .

Hopkins died in 1657 and bequeathed money to found a school dedicated to "the breeding up of hopeful youths for the public service of the country in future times." Colonial officials wanted Hopkins PERSON 's bequest to remain in Connecticut GPE and appointed three men, Davenport GPE and two others, as executors of Hopkins ORG 's will. They created the Hopkins Fund ORG , from which Hopkins Grammar School ORG was established in 1660.
The exact date of Hopkins School's ORG founding is a matter of definition. The historical record of the executors' report implies the trust was created on May 4, 1660, but since the Julian NORP calendar was in use then, the date corresponds to May 14 on modern calendars. The papers that created the fund were presented and accepted on May 30, and many use this date as the official date of the school's founding. Finally, on June 4 (June 14 on modern calendars) Davenport transferred control of the bequest to the colony, on the condition that the colony accept responsibility for the support of the school.


=== The Fallow Years ===
"The Fallow Years" is a term coined by Thomas B. Davis PERSON in his history, Chronicles of Hopkins Grammar School ORG , to describe the period from 1696 to 1853. During this time the school had difficulty finding qualified schoolmasters, and the Hopkins Fund ORG often fell short in paying them. This forced the school to take up collections to meet its payroll. Consequently, there was great turnover in schoolmasters, some staying for no more than a year. Also contributing to the problem was the establishment of the Collegiate School ORG in New Haven GPE in 1701, which drew many local academics away from Hopkins GPE , and which later became Yale University ORG .
Public opinion of Hopkins GPE and academia in general weakened the school. During this time parents wanted children who could read and write English and understand basic arithmetic, but Hopkins PERSON continued to focus on subjects that parents deemed irrelevant, such as Latin. Parents were also displeased with schoolmasters who paid little attention to struggling students, instead focusing only on the scholars. On January 12, 1713, the committee which managed the Hopkins Fund ORG began releasing £12–£15 annually to run elementary English schools in East Haven GPE and West Haven GPE . New Haven GPE stopped donating money to the Fund in 1719, which made hiring schoolmasters nearly impossible. Though the trustees of the Hopkins Fund ORG constituted an independent body, the town was known to control them with financial pressures. Richard Mansfield PERSON served as schoolmaster from 1742 to 1747, and was the last headmaster until 1839 to serve for more than three years.
Although Hopkins School ORG was still somewhat unpopular with the locals, the school moved to a new larger brick building on the Green ORG , due to the growth of New Haven GPE . Hopkins School ORG was somewhat rare among American NORP schools in that it remained open during the American Revolutionary War ORG . Former schoolmaster John Hotchkiss PERSON was killed by the British NORP in July 1779 during their invasion of New Haven GPE , and former schoolmaster Noah Williston PERSON was captured. Although the school remained open, records seem to indicate that it was frequently closed between September 1780 and October 1781, "for vacation". Shortly after the Revolution, Hopkins hired Jared Mansfield for two terms (1786–1790 and 1790–1795) to the unique position "Master of the Grammar School" to try to stabilize the school for the future. Between Mansfield GPE 's two terms, Abraham Bishop PERSON held a six-month term as headmaster during which he proposed radical reform, including making Hopkins PERSON coeducational, most of which never came to fruition. After the end of Mansfield GPE 's second term, the school returned to the pattern of short tenures for schoolmasters.
Hopkins moved buildings again in 1803 to an even larger facility near the Green ORG that took up nearly an entire block. Teachers were offered two-year contracts to teach at Hopkins, but rarely kept them. Hopkins boys grew "unruly and malicious", some roaming New Haven GPE streets at night. In 1838 the school moved once again, as the trustees believed that moving the school away from the town center would allow its students to focus more on their studies. Throughout August and September that year, they rushed through the necessary transactions to buy the new plot of land, currently the site of the Yale Law School ORG . Article: Des Moines Christian School

Des Moines Christian School

Des Moines Christian School ORG is a private Christian NORP school that opened in 1948 on the campus of First Federated Church ORG in Des Moines GPE , Iowa GPE , United States GPE . == History ==


=== Conception ===
Des Moines Christian School ORG was founded in June 1947 when forty-one people from various local churches in the greater Des Moines GPE area gathered at the Des Moines GPE YMCA to pray and consider establishing a private Christian NORP school in the Des Moines GPE area. It was decided to elect an evangelical school board, adopt a constitution, and formulate bylaws for a parent-inspired school. In 2006 the school moved to its current location in Urbandale GPE , Iowa GPE .


=== Early years ===
In 1948, classes opened in the basement of First Federated Church ORG with fifteen students and one teacher. In 1949, expansion to Grace Baptist Church ORG became necessary due to a need for more space. In 1951, both campuses consolidated and the parents decided to purchase a full city block (2.5 acres) at 62nd and Franklin Avenue and erect a school building. In September 1955, Des Moines Christian School ORG opened its doors for the first time with approximately 135 kindergarten through eighth grade students and seven teachers.


=== Expansion ===
In August 1980, Des Moines ORG Christian School ORG moved to the former Franklin Junior High School ORG building located at 48th street and Franklin Avenue, where they rented it from First Federated Church ORG , which was also occupying part of the building. In September 1983, Des Moines GPE Christian NORP added a secondary school department, which included 9th through 12 grades. In 1987, Des Moines Christian School ORG graduated twenty-nine students in its first senior high graduation ceremony. In January 1988, Des Moines GPE Christian NORP became accredited from the State of Iowa GPE for grades 7-12 (the elementary had previously received accreditation.) In September 1995, they received accreditation from the Association of Christian Schools International ORG ( ACSI ORG ). In the fall of 2005, Des Moines GPE Christian School ORG moved out of the rented space at First Federated Church ORG and into their own facility in Urbandale GPE , a suburb on the west side of Des Moines GPE .


=== Recent years ===
Starting in the 2017–18 school year, the school added a new addition, with a second gymnasium, and new rooms for the vocal and instrumental music classes. This year also introduced the split of the Secondary section into the High School and Middle School ORG - this overhaul brought sixth grade over to the Secondary area as a mix of the elementary and the middle school. This update also moved most of the high school classrooms to the second floor and the middle school classes to the main floor.
The 2018–19 school year brought a change to the high school schedule, importing a new "flexible schedule", which uses 30 "blocks" per day to create a five-day schedule. Classes consist of various amounts of blocks per day. As of the 2019–2020 school year, the high school has moved to a six-day schedule, each with 30 "blocks". This was intended to provide students with more free time during the day, and to prepare them for what a college schedule may be like.
The 2021–22 school year brought another addition to the school, when an addition to the secondary was added, which increased classroom numbers for middle and high school. In addition to this, a chapel, administrative offices, and open collaborative spaces were added. This allowed for the addition allowed for new staff to be onboarded and the student cap per grade to be increased from roughly 90 students to over 110.
The 2023–24 school year came with two new additions to the school, the first of which was a main gymnasium addition which resurfaced the floors, added a new video board, spot light system, and sound system. The other addition to the school was a 104-acre land acquisition paid for by a single donation of over $7 million. The land lays north of the current campus between NW 42nd Street and Meredith Drive PERSON and will be used as a high school once the land is developed. This land was formerly farm ground and has development plans currently being created.
Today, more than 100 different churches are represented in the school's preschool through twelfth grade. The enrollment of Des Moines Christian School ORG is over 1300 students in grades K-12. In addition, more than 200 children are enrolled in the preschool and daycare program. The school employs more than 200 people.
